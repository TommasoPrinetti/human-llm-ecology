# Supervisor Thesis Report: "Using AI is turning to a supervisor rather than someone who thinks or does"

## User Request

The researcher asked: "Find evidence for this thesis: 'Using AI is turning to a supervisor rather than someone who thinks or does.'" The task is to assess whether evidence from the EL2MP Root Vault (Ex0–Ex18) supports, partially supports, or contradicts the claim that professionals using AI shift toward a supervisory/non-doing role.

## Short Answer

The evidence **partially supports** the thesis. Multiple students across Cohorts 1 and 3 describe their primary role when using LLMs as checking, verifying, judging, directing, and meta-deciding — rather than thinking or doing. However, no student explicitly self-reports that they have "stopped thinking." The supervisory framing is consistently present but does not constitute a complete abdication of cognitive engagement.

## Conclusion: Does the Evidence Support the Thesis?

**Partial support.** The evidence consistently shows students describing their role in supervisory terms — checking, verifying, judging, directing, delegating, and meta-deciding. The verbs that appear across the evidence are overwhelmingly about oversight, not creation.

However, the evidence does not fully support the thesis for three reasons:

1. **No evidence of total abdication.** No student says "I have stopped thinking." Clara, Thérèse, and Léa all emphasize that domain knowledge remains necessary for effective supervision — thinking is diminished but not eliminated.

2. **Framework priming cannot be ruled out.** The exercise designs themselves (mock trial structures, delegation matrices, role-mapping tasks) may have primed supervisory language. Léa's "Who's the Judge? Clara" remark explicitly references the exercise framework.

3. **Counter-evidence from Béa.** Béa explicitly states she does not delegate the entire task and retains some hands-on work. This suggests the supervisory shift is partial, not total.

The thesis is **directionally correct** — the dominant pattern is a shift from doing to overseeing — but it overstates the case if interpreted as a complete replacement of thinking with supervising.
